How Well Does the House Represent Your State?

In a perfectly proportional system, the partisan split of a state's delegation in the U.S. House of Representatives would mirror the partisan split of its voters. In practice it doesn't — because of gerrymandering, geographic sorting, single-member plurality elections, and small delegation sizes.

Three views of the same question. Switch modes with the toggle below the map.

  • Party bias. Signed difference between the Democratic share of a state's House delegation and its 2024 U.S. House two-party vote, in percentage points (a +5% bias means the delegation is 5 percentage points more Democratic than the electorate; the scatter axis labels this "pp"). Blue = more Democratic than voters, red = more Republican, white = proportional.
  • Absolute fit. How close the two-party seat split is to the two-party vote split, ignoring direction. Green = close to proportional, red = far off.
  • Gallagher index. A standard disproportionality index across Democratic, Republican, and other / independent shares. Green = 0 (perfect), red = high (worse). Penalises larger gaps more heavily than absolute fit and accounts for third-party voters.

The seats slider fades states with small delegations on both the map and the scatter — a one-seat state can only be 0% or 100% Democratic, so it cannot be proportional by construction. The scatter below the map plots one dot per state (delegation size on the x-axis, deviation on the y-axis); switch modes to see how the picture changes. The national strip at the very bottom compares the nationwide U.S. House vote split with the nationwide House seat split.

Caveats: uncontested districts (where one major party has no candidate on the ballot) skew the state's two-party House vote and exaggerate the winner's share. Vacant seats are not counted toward a state's delegation size. DC and territories have no voting House representation and are omitted.
