How Well Does the House Represent Your State?

In a perfectly proportional system, the partisan split of a state's delegation in the U.S. House of Representatives would mirror the partisan split of its voters. In practice it doesn't — because of district lines, geographic sorting, single-member plurality elections, and small delegation sizes.

This map shows the representation deviation for every state: the difference between the Democratic share of its current House delegation (119th Congress) and the Democratic share of its 2024 presidential two-party vote. Blue means the delegation is more Democratic than the electorate; red means it's more Republican; white is roughly proportional. Hover or tap any state for the full breakdown.

Caveats: presidential vote share is a proxy for partisan lean, not party identification. States with one or two House seats can only land on coarse values (0%, 50%, 100%), so their deviations look extreme even when nothing unusual is happening. Vacant seats and independents are excluded from the delegation share. DC and territories have no voting House representation and are omitted.
